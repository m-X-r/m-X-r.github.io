  3. Key Points are important as they will help us build our map to tell the story. After we have our map we will make web pages for each area of our map to create our text-based RPG html game. Its best practice to complete your map before you start coding so you will have a guideline to go by, even though things can and probably will change on your map as you begin coding.
  4. Lets start with the first Key Point
    Up to first Key Point

    A Mom Pig sent her three kids off to seek their fortunes.

    To imagine this in the case of an RPG its best to start to turn the story into a map. So, imagine a grid and each box of the grid is going to be a new part of the map to your story. In this case let’s start our map at the home of this mom pig and her kids. Draw a square in the middle of your paper and inside write Mom Pigs House. To read a map you need a compass. Compasses have a N (up) South (down) East (right) and West (left). So, for this example instead of using up, down, right, and left, we are going to use North, South, East, and West to represent those directions. Lets draw a compass in the corner of our map.

    If you start at the Moms house what is N, S, E and W of the house? For this we must use our imaginations to imagine the type of place where the three little pigs grew up. To do this we can come up with something on our own or use the illustrations in the story itself. For this example, we will use the illustrations in the story itself.

    From the image it looks like the pig family lived in some rolling hills with small trees and flowers everywhere. To have a house I feel like the pigs would need Food and Water to go with their shelter. As we see later in the story there are places nearby for them to go and collect food, like the turnip field or the apple tree. That leads me to think that they would have to have someplace to store the food they collected. So, let’s say to the West of Mom Pigs House there is their Food Shed where the keep all the food they collect. Now draw a box to the west of Mom Pigs House box draw a box and call it Food Shed.

    To the East of Mom Pigs House let’s put a well so they can have fresh water. Draw another box to the East and label it Mom Pigs House Well.

    I think to the North of the house Should be a drop off giving them a lovely view of all the rolling hills and trees and such. To the North of Mom Pigs House draw a box and label it Rolling Hills Drop Off.

    Now to the South of Mom Pigs House I think should be a path to get to town, because the three little pigs need a way to get going to make that fortune. So, to the South of Mom Pigs House draw another box and label it Path to Town.

    Now we have 5 boxes; Mom Pigs House, Mom Pigs House Well, Rolling Hills Drop Off and Path to Town, as well as our Compass. Let’s expand our Map. We mostly want our story to focus on the pigs heading to town and running into the wolf. So, I think the only way to go should be the path to town. How do we do this? We need to define the N, S, E, W of all our squares and make them go "nowhere". We will start this process at Mom Pigs House Well. Mom Pigs House is to the West of the well so draw boxes for North, South, and East around Mom Pigs House Well, and inside write Cannot Pass.

    As you can see that already fills our East boxes for Rolling Hills Drop Off as well as Path to Town. Now we will draw boxes North and West of Rolling Hills Drop Off and write cannot pass in them as well.

    Here Comes the Food Shed, draw boxes to the West and South of the Food Shed, and write cannot pass inside.

    All that is left now is the South side of the Path to Town box. We want the path to go South, so for South we will draw our box and name it Path to Town 2.

    We have now finished making our map for this first Key Point of the Story!

    Since The first pig meets a Man with Straw we need to have him on the path somewhere so that the pigs can run into him. To make sure our player is forced to interact with the Man with Straw we need to make the West and East of Path to Town 2 unpassable, while making its South side a box we will call Man with Straw.

    As you can see I have used up my piece of paper. Never fear Tape is here! I’m just going to grab another sheet of paper and tape it to my map. Make sure to tape on the back so the strips of tape do not impede your drawing.

    So, When the Pigs meet the man we can add some options and some dialogue. For now, we are only focusing on the map so we will cover that option when we start making web pages. In my version of this story after the pigs pass the Man with the Straw the First Pig will want to build his house as soon as possible. So, to the South of Man with Straw draw a box and label it Path to Town 3.

    Then to the West of Path to Town 3 draw a box and call it First Pigs House.

    Next, we can fill the West and East around Man with Straw, as well as the East of Path to Town 3 with Cannot Pass. This ensures that our player more likely to enter the First Pigs House.

    Now the next part of this point, the part with the blowing the house down and eating by the wolf. We will need to wait and cover that in the text of the web pages, as it does not affect the landscape. The house may be blown down, but it’s still in the same spot, and we don’t have to go anywhere new. So, we have now finished making our map for this second Key Point of the Story!

  14. HTML is a coding language that tells web browsers how to display information. It is based on nesting, just like Russian Dolls.

    Your containers are data types, nesting inside of other data types that tell the web browser where to put things. The content of each container is the images, text, videos, etc. that make up your web page. I know, it can seem a little confusing at first.

    The first thing we will tackle is not a container at all. It is more like a sign, telling people what kind of Russian doll you are about to show them. It is called a Declaration; the declaration tells the internet browser that the document it is about to read is an HTML document and what version of HTML it is. To make our declaration go back to your waiting blank notepad document, at the top of the page type the following:

    < !Doctype html >

    Next, we need to complete the most important step, Saving! Go up to the top of the document and click File. Next go down to Save As and click it. A new window will open, this window will allow you to navigate to your threeLittlePigs folder. Name your document momPigsHouse.html and click save. Now sit back and bask in your coolness because you are a computer programmer now!

  15. Since your file is saved as an .html file, when you click on it, it will open in the web browser. If it opens in the web browser every time you click it, you may be wondering how you are going to be able to edit your file in notepad now. To open the file in notepad you will first right click on the file. Next hover over the open with option, and then select notepad. If you do not see notepad as an option, you can select the choose another app option and scroll until you see notepad.

    Now you can open and edit all your html files. If the page is open in a web browser while you are editing, don't forget to refresh your web browser after you save in notepad so you can see the changes.

  16. Now we are going to create our first container, using an HTML Element. An HTML element or container, as I will be referring to them, are defined by a start tag, some content, and an end tag. In essence:

    < tagname >...content goes here...< tagname >

    Our first "Container" or HTML Element is going to be our html container. This container encompasses everything else. The HTML tag is the largest Russian Doll. It tells the browser that everything inside it makes up the HTML page. In your notepad document go down a space below your declaration and type the following:

    <!Doctype html>
    <html>


    </html>

    Dont forget the most important step. Save!

    Notice how there is some space between our start tag <html> and our end tag </html> ? That is because we are nesting things, so we need to leave space for all the other containers that need to go in between our html starting and ending tag.

  17. Now our main document is going to consist of two big containers. Our head container and our body container. Our head container is going to mostly communicate style and our body container is going to mostly communicate substance. To add these two style and substance containers make a space below your html starting tag and indent some. Then type the following:

    <!Doctype html>
    <html>

                <head>

                </head>

                <body>


                </body>

    </html>

  19. Now we are gearing up to add a little substance to our body container. But before we do, we need to go over some basic container types to use inside our body container. Below I will show you some basic tags and the code you need to make them happen.

    • H:

      the h or heading tag creates a heading or banner. So, you have text with a space above and below. Headings go from 1-6, one being the largest and six being the smallest. It goes h1, h2, h3, h4, h5 and h6.

      <h1> H1 </h1>

      <h2> H2 </h2>

      <h3> H3 </h3>

      <h4> H4 </h4>

      <h5> H5 </h5>
      <h6> H6 </h6>

    • p:

      the paragraph container, it creates, well, a paragraph. It will block out whatever text you put in with a space above and below. Just like when using the word typing program.

This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. </p>

      <p> This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph. This is a paragraph.</p>

    • ul or ol:

      These are unordered list and ordered list containers. They both do the exact same thing with one little difference. They create a bullet list, ul aka unordered list use round bullet points. Or they create numbered list, ol, aka ordered list use numbers.

      <ul>

      • <li> this </li>
      • <li> is </li>
      • <li> an </li>
      • <li> unordered </li>
      • <li> list </li>

      </ul>


      <ol>

      2. <li> is </li>
      3. <li> an </li>
      4. <li> ordered </li>
      5. <li> list </li>

      </ol>

      The a container holds links to other places. It could be a webpage on the internet or a webpage that you made. You can even go to a different section of the webpage that you are on with the help of the id attribute. The id attribute, like the declaration tag, is less like a container and more like a sign.

    First off, adding padding. Padding can make our page look a little more professional by adding some space around the edges of our page. So the text will not run from the very beginning to the very end of our page. Most web pages have some empty space around all the edges, but right now ours does not. To add some, we need to add some tags in our head section. Go to the head section of your Mom Pigs House Page. Then make some space under your title tag and type the following:

                <head>
                            <title> Mom Pigs House </title>
                            <style> body {padding:60px;} </style>
                </head>

    Once you save and refresh your web browser, the page should look something like this:

    Notice how now the text does not run all the way across the page and how far down the header has come. That is because a 60-pixel, or 60px, area around the body container has been formed, and that is providing the extra space. You can play with the amount of padding that you want. I used 60px to ensure that you could see the difference. Add style container to the head of the Mom Pigs House Well page, along with whatever amount of padding that you decide on. Whatever amount of pixels you decide on be sure to add it in both your Mom Pigs House page as well as your Mom Pigs House Well page.

  24. Let's talk Templets and Style Sheets! I don't know about you but changing something in Mom Pigs House to style the page up and then going into Mom Pigs House well to add a few lines a code was a pain. What if we had like five pages instead of two, or five-hundred! There has got to be an easier way to change things, right? There is, and it is called a cascading style sheet, or CSS.

    CSS is used to format the layout of a webpage, or add style. The great thing about it is its one document that you can link to as many pages as you want. So, if you change one thing on the CSS document, it changes that same thing on all your pages, weather its 2, 5, or 500! To make a style sheet open a new notepad document and save it as threeLittlePigsStyles.css in your three little pigs folder with your web pages. Now remember what style we added to our Pig Moms House and Pig Moms House Well pages? We had a title tag and a style tag. Unfortunately, each page needs its own title tag, so that will remain. As far as the style tag goes we are going to transfer that info to our CSS page. At the top of your CSS page type the following:

    body {padding:60px;}

    Now complete the Most Important Step, and open your Mom Pigs House Page. in the head section replace the style tag with a link tag:

    <link rel="stylesheet" href=" ">

    Whenever you use this tag always include rel="stylesheet" or the tag will not work. For the href attribute you are going to put the file name of your style sheet in between the " ", in our case it is threeLittlePigsStyles.css. Be sure that your style sheet is saved in the same folder as your web pages. Open your Mom Pigs House Well page and add the Link tag above where the style tag is now in the head section. After you save you should now be able to reload your browser and see your Mom Pigs House and Mom Pigs House Well pages with padding around them.
